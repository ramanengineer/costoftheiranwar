Everyday families paying the price. For ordinary Iranians, this war has meant shattered hospitals, collapsed electricity grids, and fuel shortages that have halted ambulances. Iran's economy — already under sanctions — is forecast to contract by more than 10% of GDP in 2026 alone (Chatham House). That's not numbers on a spreadsheet; it's pharmacies with empty shelves, families who can't refrigerate insulin, children sitting in blacked-out classrooms.

American families feeling it at home. Gas is up $0.98 per gallon nationwide. Diesel — which moves food, goods, and construction materials — has jumped from $3.75 to $5.37/gal, a 43% rise in one month. Every sustained $10 rise in oil costs the typical U.S. household ~$450 extra per year (Moody's Analytics). Grocery prices are rising because the Strait of Hormuz controls roughly one-third of the world's fertilizer exports, and those shipments have stopped.

Global South on the edge. Debt-laden developing countries — already stretched by pandemic recovery and post-Ukraine inflation — are now rationing fuel and subsidizing energy costs. Economists warn of a potential debt crisis wave across Africa and Southeast Asia if Northern central banks are forced to raise interest rates to combat war-driven inflation.

New Zealand is not insulated. NZ imports around 75% of its liquid fuels. Petrol prices at the pump have risen sharply, hitting lower-income households hardest. NZ's trading partners — particularly China, the EU, and the US — are all at elevated recession risk, which threatens NZ export revenues. The RBNZ is watching closely as imported inflation could force a pause or reversal of planned rate cuts.
